Go deeper

In practice

Rule-based AI runs your out-of-office replies and expense-flag thresholds. Machine learning ranks your inbox, filters spam, and forecasts demand. Deep learning transcribes your meetings and unlocks your phone. Generative AI drafts your emails. You already manage with AI every day — the new part is that it now writes.

As a group · 5 min

  1. Call out tools your team touches every day — someone capture the list.
  2. Sort each one: rule-based, machine learning, or generative AI.
  3. Agree on your team’s three most AI-dependent tools and share with the room.

The core idea

AI does not follow rules a person wrote. It studies mountains of examples and writes its own.

Section 02 · Training

Machines learn by example.

Instead of being told every rule, a model is shown data until it can spot the pattern on its own. There are three main ways to teach it.

Supervised

Learn from labeled answers

Show the model thousands of emails marked "spam" or "not spam." It learns the traits that separate them, then labels new mail on its own.

Unsupervised

Find hidden structure

Hand the model raw customer data with no labels. It groups similar shoppers together, revealing segments no one defined in advance.

Reinforcement

Learn by reward

Let the model act, then reward good moves and penalize bad ones. Over millions of tries it learns to win — at games, or steering a robot.

Play · You be the ML engineer

Go deeper

In practice

  • Supervised, at work: a ticket router trained on last year’s categorized help requests.
  • Unsupervised, at work: clustering open-ended survey comments into themes nobody predefined.
  • Reinforcement, at work: a scheduling assistant that improves as invites get accepted or declined.

As a group · 5 min

  1. Pick one dataset your unit already produces as a byproduct of normal work (tickets, requests, survey comments).
  2. Decide together which of the three learning types could use it — and what it would predict.
  3. Name one thing that goes wrong if the historical examples are biased or mislabeled.

Section 03 · Architecture

Layers that learn.

A neural network passes information through layers of tiny units called neurons. Each connection has a weight — a dial the network tunes during training. Early layers catch simple features; later layers combine them into an answer.

Press Run to send a signal forward through the network, left to right, and watch each layer light up as it activates.

Ready

Quick check: During training, what actually changes inside the network?

Go deeper

In practice

No engineer writes “pointy ears = cat.” The network invents its own features by adjusting millions of weights until its guesses stop being wrong. That’s why even its builders often can’t fully explain one specific decision — the logic lives in the weights, not in readable rules. It’s also why “audit the code” is the wrong ask; “audit the training data and the outcomes” is the right one.

As a group · 5 min

  1. One person names a consequential decision AI might help make at Vanderbilt (screening, routing, flagging).
  2. Discuss: if someone challenged that decision, what would “explain it” actually require?
  3. Agree on one question your team would ask any vendor selling you an AI decision tool.

Section 04 · Language Models

It predicts the next word.

A large language model has read a huge slice of the internet and learned one skill extremely well: given some text, guess what comes next. It breaks language into tokens (word-chunks) and assigns every possible next token a probability.

Build a sentence yourself. At each step, the model offers candidates with a probability. Pick one and watch it continue.

Go deeper

In practice

Two implications for work. First: the model has no database of facts — knowledge is baked in at training time, so it can’t “look up” your org chart or yesterday’s announcement unless you paste it in. Second: it never decides to stop and check — it just keeps predicting. Tools that search the web or your documents first (“retrieval”) bolt that checking on.

On your own · 3 min

  1. Write down one task this week where you’d be tempted to use an AI answer as-is.
  2. Note what a 30-second verification of that answer would look like — the source you’d check.
  3. Keep the note. You’ll use it again in Section 07.

Section 05 · Randomness

Same question,
different answers.

A setting called temperature controls how adventurous the model is when it picks the next word. Low temperature plays it safe and repeatable. High temperature takes creative risks — and can wander off.

Drag the slider. The answer to "Why is the sky blue?" rewrites itself as the temperature climbs.

Quick check: Your team needs the exact same policy summary every time it’s generated. Which setting?

Go deeper

In practice

Match the dial to the task. Summarizing a policy, drafting minutes, extracting action items: low — you want the same right answer every time. Naming a program, brainstorming formats, writing a fresh opener: higher — you want surprises. Many tools hide the dial, but you can steer it with words: “give me the standard answer” vs. “give me ten wild options.”

On your own · 3 min

  1. On your device, open your approved AI tool.
  2. Ask the same question twice in two separate chats — something from your real work.
  3. Compare the two answers. The differences are the randomness you just learned to control.

Section 06 · Prompting

Ask precisely.

A vague question gets a vague answer. Strong prompts usually contain four parts. Hover or tap each one below to find it in the example.

You are an experienced internal-communications writer. My team of 12 moves to a new expense system next month, and several people are anxious about the change. Draft a short announcement email that explains the timeline and reassures the team in three brief paragraphs with a warm, direct tone.
Go deeper

In practice

The fastest upgrade is iteration, not perfection. Treat the first answer as a draft from a capable new hire: “shorter,” “warmer,” “as a table,” “now for an audience of deans.” The role line is what Vanderbilt’s own Dr. Jules White calls the Persona Pattern — his research shows it’s one of the highest-leverage prompt moves there is. His Coursera course (on the Watch slide) teaches a full catalog of these patterns.

On your own · 5 min

  1. Pick one real email or document you owe this week.
  2. Write a prompt for it with all four parts: role, context, task, format.
  3. Run it, then refine twice — one correction per message (“shorter,” “warmer”).
  4. If there’s time: trade screens with a neighbor and borrow one move from their prompt.

Section 07 · Reality Check

Powerful, not magic.

Genuinely good at

  • Drafting, summarizing, and rewriting text
  • Explaining concepts at any level
  • Translating and reformatting
  • Brainstorming and first drafts

Unreliable at

  • Facts, dates, and citations — verify everything
  • Exact arithmetic and counting
  • Recent events after its training cutoff
  • Knowing when it's wrong
Key term · Hallucination

When a model states something false with total confidence, that's a hallucination. It isn't lying — it's predicting a plausible-sounding next word that happens to be wrong. This is exactly why a human has to check its work.

Quick check: An AI tool cites a university policy with a specific title and effective date. What should you do?

Go deeper

In practice

A working verification habit: (1) a fact, date, number, name, or citation — check the source. (2) a summary of a document you provided — spot-check two claims against the original. (3) a draft — you’re the editor; nothing ships unread. The model is a brilliant first-draft machine and an unreliable reference librarian.

As a group · 5 min

  1. Each person shares the task they wrote down in Section 04.
  2. As a table, pick the highest-stakes one — where an unverified AI answer costs the most.
  3. Agree on the check for it and who owns that check. Write both down.

Section 08 · Responsibility

Use it well.

Bias

A model reflects the data it learned from — including society's blind spots. Its output can quietly repeat stereotypes. Read critically, especially about people.

Privacy

Anything you paste may be stored or used to improve the tool. Never share personnel records, student data, or confidential university business in unapproved tools.

Accountability

AI drafts; you decide. Disclose AI use where your unit's policy asks for it, review everything before it ships, and own the final output — the tool is never the author of record.

Which of these is the safest thing to paste into a public AI chatbot?

Go deeper

In practice

Guardrails that scale for a team: use university-approved AI tools for anything touching university data; when in doubt, treat a prompt like a public post; agree where disclosure is expected (external communications, evaluations, anything signed with your name); and keep a human accountable for every output — “the AI wrote it” is never an acceptable answer.

As a group · 7 min

  1. Draft one sentence of team policy together: “On our team, we use AI for ___, never for ___, and we disclose when ___.”
  2. Test it against the three ideas on this slide — bias, privacy, accountability. Does the sentence still hold?
  3. Nominate one person to bring the draft to your next team meeting.

Section 09 · Wellbeing

AI saves time,
if you let it.

Research says the time AI saves quietly refills — with more tasks, faster expectations, and invisible checking work. Protecting your team's gains is a management skill, not a nice-to-have. (See “AI Doesn't Reduce Work — It Intensifies It,” HBR.)

Pattern 01

Workload creep

The hour AI saves gets silently backfilled with new tasks. Efficiency becomes intensification unless someone decides — out loud — where the saved time goes.

Pattern 02

Always-on drift

When drafts take minutes instead of hours, response-time expectations quietly rise for everyone. Speed becomes a standard nobody agreed to.

Pattern 03

Invisible labor

Reviewing, verifying, and fixing AI output is real work — and it rarely shows up in workload conversations. Name it, or it gets absorbed for free.

If work feels heavier, say so

Workload creep is an organizational pattern, not a personal failing. Renegotiating scope with your manager is a professional move, not a complaint. And for support beyond your team: Vanderbilt Employee Assistance & Work-Life resources.

Go deeper

In practice

The managers who keep AI's gains do three things: they decide explicitly where saved time goes (deeper work, fewer weekend emails — not just more volume); they keep verification work visible in workload conversations; and they watch their own behavior, because a manager sending AI-fast replies at 9 p.m. resets the whole team's expectations.

On your own · 3 min

  1. Private self-check — no sharing: in the last month, did time saved by tools become breathing room, or more tasks?
  2. Have response-time expectations on your team risen without anyone deciding they should?
  3. Write one sentence: where do you want your team's next saved hour to go?
