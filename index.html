The core idea

AI does not follow rules a person wrote. It studies mountains of examples and writes its own.

Section 02 · Training

Machines learn by example.

Instead of being told every rule, a model is shown data until it can spot the pattern on its own. There are three main ways to teach it.

Supervised

Learn from labeled answers

Show the model thousands of emails marked "spam" or "not spam." It learns the traits that separate them, then labels new mail on its own.

Unsupervised

Find hidden structure

Hand the model raw customer data with no labels. It groups similar shoppers together, revealing segments no one defined in advance.

Reinforcement

Learn by reward

Let the model act, then reward good moves and penalize bad ones. Over millions of tries it learns to win — at games, or steering a robot.

Section 03 · Architecture

Layers that learn.

A neural network passes information through layers of tiny units called neurons. Each connection has a weight — a dial the network tunes during training. Early layers catch simple features; later layers combine them into an answer.

Section 04 · Language Models

It predicts the next word.

A large language model has read a huge slice of the internet and learned one skill extremely well: given some text, guess what comes next. It breaks language into tokens (word-chunks) and assigns every possible next token a probability.

Build a sentence yourself. At each step, the model offers candidates with a probability. Pick one and watch it continue.

Section 05 · Randomness

Same question,
different answers.

A setting called temperature controls how adventurous the model is when it picks the next word. Low temperature plays it safe and repeatable. High temperature takes creative risks — and can wander off.

Section 06 · Prompting

Ask precisely.

A vague question gets a vague answer. Strong prompts usually contain four parts. Hover or tap each one below to find it in the example.

You are an experienced internal-communications writer. My team of 12 moves to a new expense system next month, and several people are anxious about the change. Draft a short announcement email that explains the timeline and reassures the team in three brief paragraphs with a warm, direct tone.

Section 07 · Reality Check

Powerful, not magic.

Genuinely good at

  • Drafting, summarizing, and rewriting text
  • Explaining concepts at any level
  • Translating and reformatting
  • Brainstorming and first drafts

Unreliable at

  • Facts, dates, and citations — verify everything
  • Exact arithmetic and counting
  • Recent events after its training cutoff
  • Knowing when it's wrong
Key term · Hallucination

When a model states something false with total confidence, that's a hallucination. It isn't lying — it's predicting a plausible-sounding next word that happens to be wrong. This is exactly why a human has to check its work.

Quick check: An AI tool cites a university policy with a specific title and effective date. What should you do?

Section 08 · Responsibility

Use it well.

Bias

A model reflects the data it learned from — including society's blind spots. Its output can quietly repeat stereotypes. Read critically, especially about people.

Privacy

Anything you paste may be stored or used to improve the tool. Never share personnel records, student data, or confidential university business in unapproved tools.

Accountability

AI drafts; you decide. Disclose AI use where your unit's policy asks for it, review everything before it ships, and own the final output — the tool is never the author of record.
